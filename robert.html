About J. Robert Oppenheimer

J. Robert Oppenheimer (1904â€“1967) was an American theoretical physicist, best known for his role as the scientific director of the Manhattan Project, the U.S. project during World War II that developed the atomic bomb.

Oppenheimer is often referred to as the "father of the atomic bomb" for his leadership in the development of nuclear weapons. His work marked a turning point in history, profoundly changing global politics, science, and warfare.

Oppenheimer later became a prominent advocate for the peaceful use of atomic energy and was deeply involved in the post-war atomic policy debates. Despite his contributions, his political affiliations led to controversy, and he was stripped of his security clearance during the Red Scare.
