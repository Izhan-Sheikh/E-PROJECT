About J.J. Thomson

J.J. Thomson (1856â€“1940) was a British physicist who is best known for discovering the electron in 1897. This groundbreaking discovery revolutionized our understanding of atomic structure and laid the foundation for modern particle physics.

He developed the "plum pudding model" of the atom, which described the atom as a sphere of positive charge with negatively charged electrons embedded within it. Thomson's work also earned him the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1906 for his research on the conduction of electricity in gases.

As the director of the Cavendish Laboratory at Cambridge University, Thomson mentored several future Nobel laureates, including Ernest Rutherford. His legacy continues to shape the fields of atomic and quantum physics.
